Gerhard richeter is a German visual artist know for his photorealistic painting and photographs. He was born in Dresden in 1932 AD. Growing up under both the Nazi regime and later the strict controls of East Germany, his early life was shaped by conflict, censorship, and propaganda. These experiences would later echo through his art, where themes of memory, history, and uncertainty often resurface. He create the painting in a such detailed that it looks like a real. He is one of the most famous artist in 20 and 21st century.

In his latest interview he talks his long career and evolving approach to abstraction and realism. He explains that much of his process depends on allowing chance to step in especially in his abstract paintings, where layers of paint are applied and then partially erased. He describes his blurred photo-paintings as an attempt to capture the fleeting and unreliable nature of memory, reminding us that even the most realistic images cannot fully hold onto the past. Richter admits that he never aims to deliver a message, insisting instead that art should remain open to clearify each viewer completing the work with their own perception. Richter has become one of the world's most celebrated living artists, with his paintings breaking records at auctions. Despite his fame, Richter remains modest, often saying that his art is not about delivering truth but about creating images that allow us to question what we see and what we remember.
